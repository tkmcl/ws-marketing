## What happens when three majors cut buybacks in the same week?

BP, Equinor, and TotalEnergies all scaled back shareholder returns within ten days of each other. The market read it as capital discipline. Investors asked the obvious questions about margins and commodity outlook.

But there's something more interesting in the earnings calls if you look past the headline numbers.

Equinor's leadership talked about "high-grading"—exiting their Vaca Muerta position to concentrate capital on higher-return opportunities. Shell's CEO acknowledged a reserve replacement gap that will require careful choices about organic growth versus M&A. BP's incoming leadership inherits a portfolio where every project now faces tighter scrutiny.

None of these companies are in trouble. They're making deliberate choices about where to allocate scarce capital. And the quality of those choices will matter more in the next decade than it has in the last two.

---

**The planning challenge that doesn't get discussed enough**

When capital was abundant, portfolio prioritization could be approximate. Fund the obvious winners, delay the marginal projects, revisit next year. Most planning systems were built for that world—strong on technical analysis, weaker on portfolio-level trade-offs.

The current environment is different. Boards want to see scenario robustness, not point estimates. Investors expect clear rationale for why Project A was funded while Project B was deferred. And management teams need to respond to changing conditions faster than quarterly planning cycles allow.

The challenge isn't generating more analysis. Most organizations already have sophisticated reservoir models, detailed cost estimates, and extensive scenario libraries. The harder problem is translating all that technical depth into portfolio decisions that people actually trust.

I've seen planning processes where asset teams spend months building detailed project economics, only for the final prioritization to happen in a two-hour leadership meeting based on slides and advocacy. The analysis exists, but it doesn't flow through to the decision in a way that's transparent or auditable.

---

**What boards are actually asking for**

The questions from boards and investors have shifted. It used to be enough to show that a project had attractive returns. Now the questions are more pointed:

How does this project perform if commodity prices stay flat for three years? What do we give up by funding this instead of the alternative? If we need to cut 20% of the capital program, which projects should we defer—and can you show me the analysis behind that recommendation?

These aren't unreasonable questions. But most planning processes struggle to answer them quickly, or to show the underlying trade-offs in a way that builds confidence rather than raising more questions.

The organizations I've seen handle this well share a few characteristics. They can show decision-makers a genuine range of outcomes, not just a base case with optimistic and pessimistic bookends. They can trace how individual project assumptions flow through to portfolio-level impacts. And they can update their analysis when conditions change without starting from scratch.

---

**The opportunity in being forced to choose**

Capital discipline sounds like constraint. It is. But it's also an opportunity to build decision-making capability that competitors lack.

When everyone has to make harder choices, the quality of those choices creates separation. The company that can quickly identify which projects are resilient across scenarios has an advantage over one that's still arguing about assumptions. The leadership team that can show investors a clear capital allocation rationale builds trust that translates into lower cost of capital.

This isn't about having more data or running more scenarios. It's about making the complexity navigable—giving decision-makers enough visibility into trade-offs that they can act with confidence rather than defaulting to politics or inertia.

---

**A question worth asking**

If your capital budget was cut by 20% next quarter, how long would it take your planning process to tell you—with confidence—which projects to defer?

Not which ones are politically safe to cut. Which deferrals would actually optimize long-term value given your strategic priorities, execution constraints, and risk tolerance.

For most organizations, the honest answer is "longer than we'd like." That gap between the question and a trustworthy answer is where real improvement happens.

The companies that close it won't just survive the capital discipline era. They'll use it to pull ahead.
