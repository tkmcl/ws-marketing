# Agent CMO Review: AIM ICP Framework
*WhiteSpace Solutions — AIM (AI-assisted Well Trajectory & Field Development Planning)*
*Review by Agent CMO — Feb 25, 2026*

---

## Bottom Line Up Front

The framework has solid bones — the trigger logic and buying committee awareness are better than average — but it is too permissive at the top (firmographics too wide), too shallow in the middle (technical readiness), and missing three entire dimensions that will define actual win rate.

---

## 1. What Is Strong

**The Operational Triggers section is the best work.** These are real moments when budgets move. "Post-mortem on a costly planning decision" and "leadership change" are genuinely good buying signals that show understanding of the O&G decision cycle, not just the org chart.

**The operator-vs-service-company distinction is correctly drawn.** Many early-stage products waste cycles on service companies thinking they are adjacent buyers. They are not — different economics, different incentives.

**"AIM augments rather than replaces domain tools"** as a technical readiness criterion is smart. It signals awareness of category entry barriers and change management risk.

**Buying committee role identification is above average** for an early ICP. Most frameworks stop at champion and forget the economic buyer entirely.

---

## 2. What Is Weak, Missing, or Wrong

### Firmographics — Too Loose, Wrong Proxies

**"$1B+ revenue" is a lazy proxy.** A $5B company with one legacy brownfield asset has zero need for AIM. A $400M independent with three active NFDPs in the North Sea is a perfect fit. Revenue correlates weakly with planning complexity. Replace with asset-level metrics: number of wells in active planning cycle, or acreage with proved undeveloped reserves.

**"5+ active FDPs"** is the right instinct but unverifiable from outside — you cannot prospect off this from public data. You need proxy signals: drilling permit filings, rig contracts, published field development plans in licensing documentation, JV announcements. If your sales team cannot operationalize a criterion, it is not a criterion.

**"English-speaking or strong international ops teams"** papers over a major strategic decision: **NOCs.** Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, Petrobras, Equinor, CNOOC represent the majority of global FDP activity. Are they in scope or out? If out, say why explicitly. If in, they need their own sub-profile with different procurement dynamics (political, relationship-driven, multi-year tender cycles).

**No anti-ICP definition.** Who should you explicitly NOT pursue? PE-backed single-asset companies? Junior explorers with no development pipeline? Companies mid-contract with SLB Delfi? This is as important as the positive profile.

---

### Operational Triggers — Lagging vs Leading

Several triggers are **lagging indicators** — arriving after the pain, not before the decision. A "post-mortem on a costly planning decision" means the mistake already happened. You want to be present when the pain is forming.

**Missing as leading indicators:**
- Drill-or-drop commitment deadlines on licenses (creates hard planning urgency)
- Announced FID timelines — public record, e.g. "FID targeted for Q3 2027" = 12-18 month window open now
- New basin entry where historical analogs do not exist (maximally uncertain = maximally valuable tool)
- ESG/Scope 3 disclosure requirements tightening (need to demonstrate optimization rigor to regulators and investors)
- Announced rig contract or drilling campaign start (FDP must be locked before then)

**Capital discipline is double-edged.** It frequently means software freeze, FTE cuts, and "let us use what we have." The version that works is: budget pressure + visible inefficiency in the current planning process = urgency to replace consultant spend with software. Those are two conditions, not one.

---

### Technical Readiness — Too Vague to Be Useful

This section describes a minimum floor that almost every operator clears, not a profile that differentiates.

"Existing subsurface data" — every E&P operator has this.

"Some digital maturity" is unactionable. Measurable proxies:
- Do they have a Chief Digital Officer (not just CIO)?
- Are they on cloud Petrel or legacy on-prem?
- Have they published a digital transformation strategy in the last 24 months?
- Have they hired data scientists into their subsurface teams?

**The data integration question is entirely absent — critical omission.** AIM is only as good as the data fed into it. On-prem data lakes with siloed systems = 6-12 month integration before AIM adds value. Cloud-native data architecture = weeks. This should be an explicit qualifier.

"Not fully outsourced to service co" undersells the issue. The real question is what percentage of planning workflow sits inside SLB/HAL engineers today. 60% SLB-embedded = conflict-of-interest problem regardless of who owns the software seat.

---

### Economic Fit — Missing Price and Budget Mechanics

"Willingness to pay for software vs. consultants" is backwards. Companies paying consultants heavily are *better* prospects — you can build an ROI story against consultant spend. The actual disqualifier is companies who expect everything to come via embedded service engineers.

**No price context.** Is AIM $200K/year? $2M/year? This changes what "$500M capex" means as a threshold.

**Budget code matters enormously in O&G.** Opex (IT/software) or capex (project budget)? Different approval authorities, different cycle timings. Capex-funded deals can move faster because they are tied to a project with its own budget.

**Budget timing is missing.** O&G companies typically freeze annual budgets in Q3-Q4. A company not in next year's budget needs either a capex exception or a 12-month wait.

---

### Buying Committee — Incomplete, Missing the Real Killers

**Procurement and legal are absent.** In enterprise O&G, procurement kills more software deals than technical objections. Preferred vendor lists, framework agreements, minimum bid requirements.

**IT Security review is missing.** Post-2023, every major operator has an AI governance policy and security review for new software handling sensitive subsurface data. This can add 3-6 months to a sales cycle.

**The SLB/HAL blocker is undersold.** Enterprise framework agreements often have preferred vendor clauses or exclusivity provisions. This belongs in the anti-ICP criteria, not buried as a footnote.

**Economic buyer power varies by company size.** VP Subsurface at a 400-person independent can sign off on $500K. VP Subsurface at Shell is a budget holder for a function, not a procurement authority. These are different sales motions entirely.

## 3. Data Needed to Validate Each Dimension

| Dimension | What You Need |
|---|---|
| Firmographics | Win/loss analysis by company type and size — where have you actually closed? NOC vs IOC split? Median asset size? |
| Triggers | Customer discovery interviews: "What happened in the 6 months before you decided to look for a solution like this?" |
| Technical Readiness | Implementation post-mortems: what predicted fast time-to-value vs stalled deployments? Data readiness? Integration complexity? |
| Economic Fit | Closed-won: what was the budget code? Who signed? Closed-lost: budget, technical, or political? |
| Buying Committee | Champion debrief on won deals: who was actually decisive, who nearly killed it? Not the org chart — the real influence map. |

If not enough deals have closed to run this analysis, the ICP is a hypothesis, not a framework. Label it as such and build in validation checkpoints at deals 5, 10, 20.

## 4. ICP Dimensions Missing Entirely

### Competitive Displacement Profile
Profile the current vendor relationship specifically:
- Which contract cycle? When does it renew?
- Cloud-native Delfi or legacy on-prem? (Cloud-native = harder to displace; on-prem = active migration opportunity)
- Failed SLB/HAL implementation in last 3 years? (Dissatisfaction signal)
- Vendor rationalization posture or best-of-breed posture?

### Strategic Urgency Score: Existential vs. Nice-to-Have
Is AIM solving a problem with a hard deadline and existential stakes — a FID decision that defines asset economics for 20 years — or is it an optimization on a workflow that is already working? The former creates real buying urgency. The latter creates eternal POC cycles.

### Proof-of-Concept Appetite
Can this company commit to a 3-month, $50-150K pilot? That requires: available budget, an identified project to run it on, and a named internal sponsor who will champion the output.

### Change Management Track Record
Has this company successfully rolled out new technical software in the last 3 years? Do they have an internal digital adoption team? The Equinor/Johan Sverdrup reference is instructive — Equinor has the internal capability to adopt complex tools. Many operators do not.

## 5. Most Common ICP Mistakes for Products Like This

**Mistake 1: The reachable universe trap.** The described profile covers roughly 200-300 companies globally. But the actual near-term ICP — companies with active FDPs, $500M+ capex at stake, not locked into SLB enterprise contracts, with adequate data architecture and a funded budget — is probably 30-50 companies. Size it honestly.

**Mistake 2: ICP as aspiration, not description.** Start with pattern recognition from closed deals, then extrapolate. If not enough deals closed yet, interview your 3 best pilots and 3 most painful rejections.

**Mistake 3: Treating the whole account as the buyer.** Equinor E&P Norway is different from Equinor Americas is different from Equinor New Energy. Specify the organizational unit, not just the parent company.

**Mistake 4: Ignoring timing in the sales cycle.** A perfect-fit company that is 2 months into a new annual budget cycle with no software line item is a 10-month opportunity, not a 90-day close. Timing is a dimension of fit.

**Mistake 5: Confusing category education with sales qualification.** If your champion cannot articulate the problem AIM solves without your help, they are not a champion — they need category education. Different motions required.

---

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Get AIM product grounding first.** Until you know the actual price point, typical implementation timeline, and minimum data requirements, the economic fit and technical readiness sections are guesswork.

2. **Run a structured win/loss analysis** on AIM deals closed and lost to date.

3. **Define your anti-ICP explicitly** before finalizing. Who have you lost to, who pulled out, who was a bad-fit customer?

4. **Build two sub-profiles:** IOC/Large Independent (Equinor-type — known quantity) and NOC (longer cycle, different stakeholders, higher ceiling). These are different sales motions.

5. **Add a scoring model.** Convert the ICP into a 1-5 score per dimension with explicit criteria at each level. This makes it operational for SDRs and account executives.
